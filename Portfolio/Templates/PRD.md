Overview:
📌 Instructions: This section serves as a high-level summary, providing a quick understanding of the project's purpose and scope. Think of it as the executive summary for the entire document. Briefly touch on the problem being solved, the desired business outcome, the proposed solution, and why this is a priority now. Limit this section to 2-3 concise paragraphs, as you’ll dive deeper into these topics in the following sections. A sample PRD can be found here. 


Problem:
📌 Instructions: This section explains the "why" behind the project, detailing the real-world consequences for both users and the business. Describe the core user problem from the customer's perspective, highlighting the specific pain points and how the current situation makes their job harder. Outline the business impacts, such as lost customers or competitive disadvantage, and clearly articulate the cost of inaction by explaining what will happen if the problem is not addressed.


Goals:
📌 Instructions: This section defines what the product or feature aims to achieve for both the customer and Motive’s business. It should describe what success looks like for the customer once the problem is solved. Include the key business objectives, showing how the project supports broader company goals such as increasing retention or driving specific usage metrics. Then connect these target outcomes to specific, measurable success metrics (KPIs) that will be used to evaluate performance post-launch. These goals should be realistic, trackable, and clearly tied with business impact.


Key Insights & Analysis:
📌 Instructions: This section presents the data, research, and qualitative feedback that informed your decisions. Include qualitative feedback like customer quotes, quantitative data such as usage metrics, and a brief competitive analysis. It's also crucial to explicitly state any key assumptions.


Requirements:
📌 Instructions: This section serves as the definitive blueprint for a new product or feature, ensuring all stakeholders are aligned on what is in-scope of being built. To effectively complete this section, think about the key user scenarios, including the primary "happy path" and all potential edge cases. Complete the table below with a comprehensive list of all functional requirements, breaking down complex features into manageable components. Consider non-functional requirements, such as performance, security, scalability, and accessibility, and consider how the feature will behave across different platforms. Explicitly state any out-of-scope items as well.
